JOHN MILTON-ABOUT THE AUTHOR

John Milton (1608 to 1674)

John Milton was an influential English poet, intellectual
and civil servant best known for his epic poem "Paradise Lost.
"He is regarded as one of the greatest writers in the English language
with his works exploring profound themes such as religion, politics, and the human condition.

Early Life (1608-1632)

Born on December 9, 1608, in London, England, to John Milton Sr., a scrivener and composer, and Sara Jeffrey.
Milton was highly educated, attending St. Paul’s School and later Christ’s College, Cambridge
where he received a Master of Arts degree in 1632.
From a young age, he exhibited exceptional literary talent and a deep interest in theology, philosophy, and
languages, mastering Latin, Greek, and Hebrew.

Early Career and Travels (1632-1640)

After leaving Cambridge, Milton retreated to his family estate in Buckinghamshire
to focus on his studies and literary ambitions. He wrote significant early works during this period, including:
"L'Allegro" and "Il Penseroso" (pastoral poems exploring joy and contemplation).
"Comus" (a masque promoting virtue).
"Lycidas" (a pastoral elegy).
In 1638-1639, Milton traveled to Italy, meeting notable intellectuals and artists, including Galileo.
His travels greatly influenced his later work.

Political Career and Pamphleteering (1640-1660)

Milton became involved in the English Civil War, supporting the Parliamentarians against King Charles I.
A staunch Puritan, he wrote extensively on issues such as freedom of speech, divorce, and republicanism.
Notable prose works include:
"Areopagitica" (1644): A powerful defense of free speech and press.
He served as Secretary for Foreign Tongues under Oliver Cromwell’s government,
using his linguistic skills to write propaganda and international correspondence.

Blindness and Later Life (1652-1660)

Milton became completely blind in 1652, likely due to glaucoma. Despite this,
he continued to write, dictating his works to assistants, including his daughters.
His blindness inspired some of his most personal and poignant poems, including "On His Blindness."

Restoration Period (1660-1674)

Following the Restoration of Charles II in 1660, Milton faced persecution for his political involvement but avoided execution.
During this period, he focused on his most significant literary works:
"Paradise Lost" (1667): An epic poem exploring humanity’s fall from grace and themes of free will, obedience, and redemption.
"Paradise Regained" (1671): A sequel focusing on Christ’s temptation in the wilderness.
"Samson Agonistes" (1671): A dramatic poem reflecting Milton’s personal struggles, including his blindness.

Death (1674)

Milton died on November 8, 1674, in London, likely of complications from gout.
He was buried in St. Giles Cripplegate Church.

Legacy

John Milton is celebrated for his intellectual depth, mastery of language, and ability
to weave profound theological and philosophical themes into his works.
His poetry and prose have influenced countless writers and thinkers,
solidifying his place as one of the greatest figures in English literature.
